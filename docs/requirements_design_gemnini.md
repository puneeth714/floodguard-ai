Product Requirements and Architectural Specification: Flood-Guard AI
1. Executive Summary and Strategic Vision
Modern urban ecosystems, particularly rapidly expanding metropolises across the APAC region such as Bengaluru, face escalating vulnerabilities to climate-induced anomalous weather events. Frequent and intense monsoon precipitation consistently overwhelms legacy municipal infrastructure, resulting in severe urban flooding. This phenomenon disrupts critical transportation arteries, inflicts extensive property damage, and endangers human life. Traditional municipal disaster response mechanisms remain inherently reactive, relying on post-event mobilization rather than preemptive intelligence. Transforming the vast volumes of structured and unstructured data generated by public services, environmental monitoring, and citizen feedback into actionable intelligence remains a formidable technical challenge.

Flood-Guard AI is conceived as a comprehensive, AI-powered Decision Intelligence Platform designed to fulfill the objectives of the GenAI APAC Hackathon. The platform synthesizes disparate municipal data streams, real-time weather telemetrics, and high-resolution topological data to deliver predictive, personalized, and actionable intelligence. By leveraging the Google Cloud generative AI ecosystem—specifically utilizing the Agent Development Kit (ADK), Vertex AI, AlloyDB, BigQuery, Cloud Run, and the Maps Agentic UI Toolkit—the platform transitions urban disaster management from reactive chaos to proactive resilience. This document outlines the exhaustive product requirements, architectural design, algorithmic implementations, required datasets, and a strategic four-day execution plan for developing Flood-Guard AI.

2. Functional Requirements and User Personas
Flood-Guard AI is architected with a bifurcated interface that caters to two primary user profiles, dynamically toggled within the application architecture. The platform must support complex, real-world scenarios, shifting seamlessly between individual citizen navigation and macro-level municipal resource allocation.

2.1 The Resident Journey: Proactive Protection and Navigation
The resident interface prioritizes hyper-local, personalized safety analytics and dynamic navigation, adapting to the escalating severity of a weather event. The user experience is designed around a continuous narrative of prevention, navigation, emergency response, and recovery.

During the prevention phase, before significant water accumulation occurs, the system utilizes generative AI to push context-aware alerts. For example, a resident—conceptualized for this specification as "Rajesh"—might query the platform regarding the safety of his home during a morning of heavy rain. The AI leverages historical flooding data and real-time precipitation forecasts to generate a probabilistic risk assessment. The system might output a warning indicating a 72% probability of basement flooding based on the specific property's altitude and the forecasted rainfall, accompanied by a personalized, generative AI-crafted five-minute evacuation checklist. This capability requires conversational querying integrated with a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture running over local building data and municipal flood histories.

As the situation escalates into the navigation phase, standard routing algorithms become inadequate because they are generally unaware of transient water accumulation. If the resident needs to navigate the city—for instance, traveling on a two-wheeler with elderly dependents to reach the airport—the AI must analyze real-time and predictive data to recommend the safest route. The system must explicitly avoid inundated topographies and calculate altitude-aware alternatives. The recommendation might advise avoiding a historically vulnerable junction like the Silk Board, suggesting an alternate route that adds travel time but significantly reduces flood risk exposure. This requires advanced map visualization and deep personalized context awareness.   

In the event of a full-scale family emergency where a residential colony becomes flooded, the platform must support distressed individuals who are trapped. Conceptualizing a scenario where a second user, "Radha," is stuck at home, the platform must dynamically display safe gathering points and localized risk heatmaps. Crucially, this phase relies heavily on multimodal AI. The user must be able to upload a photograph or video of rising water levels. The AI, utilizing vision models, assesses the severity of the water depth and automatically triggers a priority distress signal, logging the user's location as a hostage alert point to coordinate migration and rescue.

2.2 The City Official and RWA Command Center
The administrative interface serves as a comprehensive command center for City Officials and Resident Welfare Associations (RWAs), providing macro-level decision intelligence and resource management capabilities.

The dashboard must provide real-time situational awareness through an aggregated spatial heatmap that overlays active distress signals, real-time traffic bottlenecks, and flood severity indices. This allows authorities to visualize the exact number of stranded individuals and the availability of rescue teams. The platform utilizes agentic intelligence to recommend priority actions, such as directing the first available rescue team to the most vulnerable community based on aggregated multimodal distress signals and vulnerability scoring. Furthermore, the system must generate real-time traffic diversion protocols to route emergency vehicles away from flooded areas.

A core feature of the official dashboard is the AI-powered what-if simulator for pre-flooding mitigation. Officials can interact with a conversational analytics interface to model various infrastructural interventions. An official might query the expected effect of installing a new mobile water pump at specific GPS coordinates or the impact of desilting three specific storm water drains before the monsoon peak. The AI evaluates these queries against deterministic hydrological models, topographical data, and municipal drainage capacities to show predicted impacts with associated confidence scores.

Following the acute phase of the disaster, the platform must facilitate post-flood recovery. For officials, the system generates ready-to-share briefing reports containing post-recovery recommendations, such as the calculated risk reduction of installing permanent pumps or upgrading specific drains. For citizens, the system provides proactive, personalized protection advice, suggesting optimal locations for implementing community drains—supported by visual markers generated from uploaded videos—and offering guidance on health precautions and insurance claims.

3. Data Engineering and Ingestion Strategy
A robust predictive platform is entirely dependent on the quality, latency, and granularity of its underlying data. Flood-Guard AI necessitates the assimilation of several diverse datasets spanning topography, meteorology, and municipal infrastructure.

3.1 Historical and Municipal Flood Data
Bengaluru's municipal body, the Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike (BBMP), maintains extensive records of historically vulnerable low-lying areas. Existing datasets classify numerous urban zones into categories of severe and moderate vulnerability. The platform will ingest the BBMP Low Lying Areas and Flood Prone Locations KML datasets. These datasets provide the baseline GPS coordinates and historical context for areas frequently paralyzed by water stagnation. Additionally, data regarding the 860-kilometer network of storm water drains (SWDs) and interconnected lake systems will be integrated to model drainage capacities and identify critical choke points where desilting is urgently required.   

3.2 Topographical and Elevation Data
Understanding the flow and accumulation of water relies on precise digital elevation models (DEM). The system will integrate the USGS SRTMGL1_003 dataset via Google Earth Engine (GEE), which provides global 30-meter resolution digital elevation models. By clipping this data to the Bengaluru bounding box, the platform can dynamically calculate surface slope and terrain sinks. This data is critical for determining gravity-driven water flow, identifying localized depressions prone to stagnation, and executing the spatial algorithms necessary for altitude-aware routing.   

3.3 Real-Time Meteorological Telemetrics
To transition from historical mapping to real-time prediction, the platform requires live precipitation data. The Karnataka State Natural Disaster Monitoring Centre (KSNDMC) operates over 6,000 Telemetric Rain Gauges (TRGs) and 750 Telemetric Weather Stations (TWSs) that transmit hyper-local data, including temperature, humidity, and rainfall intensity, every 15 minutes. For the purpose of hackathon development, the open-source Open-Meteo APIs will serve as robust proxies. These APIs provide high-resolution hourly precipitation, soil moisture, and surface water runoff data, utilizing models like the ECMWF IFS at a 9-kilometer resolution.   

3.4 Official Guidelines and Unstructured Community Data
To provide actionable and legally sound recommendations, the platform's generation capabilities must be grounded in official policy. Disaster Management Plans, such as the Karnataka State Disaster Management Plan (KSDMP) and BBMP flood mitigation guidelines, will be vectorized and stored. This ensures that the AI's recommendations for structural solutions align with officially sanctioned protocols. Furthermore, gated communities generate vast amounts of unstructured data via messaging applications like WhatsApp, documenting localized issues such as plumbing failures or minor waterlogging. The platform will harvest this unstructured text, applying natural language processing to categorize, track, and prioritize pending maintenance works specific to each gated community.

4. Artificial Intelligence and Multi-Agent Architecture
Flood-Guard AI requires complex, multi-step reasoning that a single Large Language Model prompt cannot reliably handle. The architecture will employ Google's open-source Agent Development Kit (ADK) to construct a deterministic, graph-based, multi-agent workflow. ADK facilitates the creation of specialized teams of AI agents that collaborate, delegate tasks, and maintain predictable execution paths, which is critical for an enterprise-grade disaster resilience platform.   

4.1 The Orchestrator Agent
The Orchestrator Agent functions as the primary cognitive router. When a user or official submits a natural language query, the Orchestrator evaluates the intent and delegates the task to the appropriate specialized sub-agent. If a resident asks for navigation out of a flooded area, the Orchestrator routes the request to the Geospatial Reasoning Agent. If an official asks for the procedural impact of deploying a pump, the request is routed to the Policy and Mitigation Agent. This orchestration ensures that specialized models with narrow responsibilities handle specific domains, increasing overall system reliability.   

4.2 The Geospatial Reasoning Agent
The Geospatial Reasoning Agent is responsible for interfacing with mapping APIs and topographical databases. When tasked with finding a safe route, this agent executes the complex logic required to bypass flooded zones. It dynamically calculates the intersection of proposed routes with active flood polygons and requests alternative paths. It also evaluates experimental route modifiers, such as identifying flyovers, to prioritize elevated traversal. This agent translates raw coordinate data into human-readable navigation instructions formatted for the frontend interface.   

4.3 The Policy and Mitigation Agent
The Policy and Mitigation Agent serves the City Official and RWA dashboards. It utilizes the Model Context Protocol (MCP) to securely connect to enterprise data stored in BigQuery and AlloyDB. This agent relies heavily on Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). When asked to generate a post-flood recovery brief, it queries the Vertex AI Search data store to retrieve relevant sections of the KSDMP and combines this with real-time incident data to synthesize a highly accurate, confidence-scored recommendation.   

4.4 The Multimodal Vision Agent
During the emergency phase, residents may upload images or videos of their surroundings. The Multimodal Vision Agent utilizes models like Gemini 1.5 Pro to process these visual inputs. It is trained to estimate water depth against common reference objects (e.g., vehicles, doorways) and classify the severity of the localized flooding. This visual assessment is converted into a structured risk score, which is immediately appended to the resident's distress signal and forwarded to the official dashboard.

5. Backend Infrastructure and Database Schema
The backend architecture leverages the Google Cloud ecosystem to guarantee scalability, low-latency inference, and seamless integration between transactional databases, data warehouses, and generative AI services.

5.1 AlloyDB AI: Operational and Vector Storage
AlloyDB AI, a fully managed PostgreSQL-compatible database, serves as the primary operational data store. AlloyDB is uniquely positioned for this platform because it natively integrates high-performance vector processing alongside standard relational data. Utilizing the pgvector extension combined with Google's Scalable Nearest Neighbors (ScaNN) index, AlloyDB enables high-throughput vector similarity searches. The ScaNN index scales to over 10 billion vectors and provides significantly faster filtered vector search queries than standard HNSW indexes.   

A critical feature of AlloyDB AI is its ability to generate inline embeddings. Through the google_ml_integration extension, the database can call Vertex AI text-embedding models directly via SQL queries (e.g., using the embedding() function). When unstructured data from a gated community's WhatsApp group is ingested, it is written to a relational table in AlloyDB. The database automatically generates a vector representation of the complaint, allowing the Policy Agent to instantly cluster similar issues (e.g., aggregating multiple reports of a blocked drain near a specific gate) using a SQL query that combines standard WHERE clauses with vector similarity operators.   

5.2 BigQuery: Analytics and Predictive Modeling
While AlloyDB handles real-time operational data, BigQuery serves as the enterprise data warehouse for massive-scale historical analytics and predictive modeling. BigQuery will store years of historical weather data, past flood incidents, and municipal infrastructure logs. Utilizing BigQuery ML, the platform can train predictive analytics models using standard SQL to forecast long-term infrastructure degradation and identify statistically significant correlations between specific rainfall intensities and localized flooding events across different wards.   

5.3 Vertex AI Search (Agent Builder)
For unstructured PDF documents, such as the extensive municipal guidelines, disaster management plans, and official policy manuals, Vertex AI Search will be utilized as a fully managed grounding data store. Vertex AI Search provides out-of-the-box chunking, semantic retrieval, and grounding for the LLMs. By pointing the ADK agents to this data store, the platform ensures that generated recommendations are strictly anchored to official policies, effectively mitigating the risk of AI hallucinations and providing necessary citations for all administrative output.   

5.4 Cloud Run and Scalable Deployment
The entire backend microservices architecture, including the Python-based ADK agents and API gateways, will be containerized and deployed on Cloud Run. As a fully managed compute environment, Cloud Run allows the application to automatically scale horizontally to handle sudden spikes in traffic—a critical requirement during an active disaster event when thousands of residents may simultaneously query the system for safe routes or submit distress signals.

6.2 Dynamic Geospatial Routing and Polygon Avoidance
A significant technical challenge inherent in the Google Maps Compute Routes API is that the routeModifiers property does not natively support avoiding dynamic, user-defined polygons (such as a rapidly expanding flood zone). While avoiding specific polygons is standard in alternative services like the HERE API, remaining within the Google ecosystem requires a sophisticated algorithmic workaround combining Google Maps and spatial analysis libraries.   

Dynamic Polygon Definition: The AlloyDB backend continuously calculates a bounding box (polygon) of actively flooded areas based on real-time FVI calculations and aggregated user SOS multimodal reports.

Alternative Route Generation: When a user requests navigation, the Geospatial Reasoning Agent calls the computeRoutes endpoint, explicitly setting computeAlternativeRoutes: true to request up to three alternative paths between the origin and destination. The API request utilizes X-Goog-FieldMask to extract the routes.polyline.encodedPolyline to minimize payload latency.   

Polyline Decoding and Spatial Intersection: The backend decodes the returned encoded polylines into standard GeoJSON LineString coordinate arrays. The system then utilizes Turf.js—a powerful geospatial analysis library—to run booleanPointInPolygon or lineIntersect functions, evaluating the generated routes against the active flood polygons.   

Route Filtering and Prioritization: Any alternative route where the Turf.js lineIntersect function returns true is immediately discarded as unsafe.

Flyover Optimization: For the surviving routes, the system leverages an experimental feature of the Routes API specific to India: flyover_info. By including "extraComputations": ["FLYOVER_INFO_ON_POLYLINE"] in the request, the API identifies elevated road segments. The routing algorithm prioritizes paths with higher percentages of flyover traversal, as elevated structures inherently bypass surface-level inundation.   

Fallback Waypoint Injection: If all generated alternative routes intersect the flood zone, the agent utilizes a spatial algorithm to calculate a safe intermediate waypoint situated outside the flooded polygon. It then issues a secondary computeRoutes request, forcing the route to pass through this calculated safe waypoint, thereby guaranteeing a detour around the hazard.   

7. User Interface and Experience (UI/UX) Design
The user interface must convey complex geospatial and predictive data without overwhelming users experiencing acute stress. The application features a simple, prominent toggle mechanism to switch between the "Resident View" and the "Official/RWA Dashboard."

7.1 Maps Agentic UI Toolkit (MAUI) Integration
To prevent the delivery of dense text blocks and provide a highly interactive experience, the frontend will integrate the experimental Maps Agentic UI Toolkit (MAUI). MAUI provides Lit-based Web components (A2UI) and specific LLM system instructions that allow the AI agent to dynamically render interactive map surfaces directly within the conversational interface.   

When the Geospatial Reasoning Agent calculates a safe route, rather than returning a list of text directions, MAUI allows the agent to return an <a2ui-surface> component. This component renders an inline map displaying the origin, destination, the calculated polyline avoiding the flood zone, and specific markers for flyovers. Similarly, when Radha asks for safe gathering points, the agent renders a place detail card indicating the altitude and capacity of the nearest elevated community hall. This visual intelligence layer keeps users engaged within the chat context and reduces cognitive overload during emergencies.   

7.2 The Command Dashboard Visualization
The Official Dashboard will utilize the Google Maps JavaScript API to render a comprehensive city-wide view. Heatmap layers will visualize the intensity of the Vulnerability Index across different wards. Custom markers will aggregate distress signals, color-coded by the severity assessed by the Multimodal Vision Agent. A side panel will host the conversational analytics interface, allowing officials to type what-if scenarios (e.g., "Effect of desilting these 3 drains") and view the resulting impact directly overlaid on the map visualization.

9. Non-Functional Requirements: Responsible AI and Explainability
As a platform dealing with public safety and disaster response, Flood-Guard AI must adhere to strict non-functional requirements regarding reliability, privacy, and explainability.

The critical path for geospatial routing—which involves invoking the Compute Routes API, decoding polylines, executing Turf.js intersection calculations, and querying AlloyDB—must resolve with minimal latency to ensure user safety and UX fluidity during rapidly evolving crisis scenarios. ADK agents must utilize asynchronous execution for secondary tasks, such as logging metrics and vectorizing complaints, to prevent blocking the primary conversational thread.

In adherence to responsible AI principles, all decisions generated by the Policy and Mitigation Agent for city officials must include explicit citations and confidence scores. When the AI recommends deploying pumps to a specific coordinate, it must expose the underlying hydrological reasoning (e.g., "Recommendation based on a predicted 40% reduction in water levels, derived from local topological sink data and current 15mm/hr rainfall loading, cross-referenced with BBMP mitigation protocols"). Furthermore, unstructured data ingested from community WhatsApp groups must undergo rigorous PII (Personally Identifiable Information) scrubbing prior to vectorization in AlloyDB to guarantee resident data privacy.

By strategically overcoming API limitations through advanced spatial analysis and deeply integrating the latest advancements in Google Cloud's agentic ecosystem, Flood-Guard AI bridges the gap between raw meteorological data and human-centric decision intelligence. The execution of this detailed architectural roadmap will yield a highly robust platform capable of demonstrating how generative AI can fundamentally alter the safety and resilience of urban populations facing the escalating threat of climate-induced disasters.
